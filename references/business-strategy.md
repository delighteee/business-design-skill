# Business Strategy

## When to Use
- Defining a product's positioning and competitive approach
- Helping a team decide what to build — and what not to build
- Articulating design trade-offs in strategic language for executives
- Evaluating whether a product is truly differentiated or just incrementally better

---

## What Strategy Is (and Isn't)

**Strategy is NOT:**
- A goal ("we want to be #1") — goals say why; strategy also says how
- A set of best practices — those can be copied by any competitor
- Simply a plan — a plan without competitive advantage is not a strategy

**Strategy IS:**
- A series of deliberate trade-off decisions
- Choosing what to do AND explicitly choosing what NOT to do
- A quest for sustainable competitive advantage
- About being genuinely *different*, not just incrementally better
- Focusing resources on the most critical leverage points

> "The essence of strategy is choosing what not to do." — Michael Porter

As a business designer, your job is to make trade-offs visible — and design for the chosen position, not for everyone.

## Porter's Three Competitive Strategies

**Cost Leadership** — Win by offering the lowest prices through operational efficiency and scale.
Design implication: simplicity, standardization, frictionless self-service, low support costs.
Examples: IKEA, Walmart, McDonald's, budget airlines.

**Differentiation** — Win by being uniquely valuable; charge a premium because customers can't get this elsewhere.
Design implication: exceptional experience, distinctive brand, features that competitors can't easily copy.
Examples: Apple, BMW, Four Seasons, Stripe, Dyson.

**Focus** — Win by serving a specific customer segment better than anyone else, either through cost focus or differentiation focus within that niche.
Design implication: deep research within the segment; ruthlessly resist scope creep into adjacent segments.
Examples: Porsche, LinkedIn, specialist B2B software.

**The strategic sin:** Getting stuck in the middle — trying to be both low-cost and highly differentiated without committing to either. This produces mediocrity.

## The Playing to Win Framework (Roger Martin)

A more actionable strategy framework for product teams. Strategy is five interconnected choices:

1. **Winning Aspiration** — What are you ultimately trying to achieve? What does "winning" look like for your product?
2. **Where to Play** — Which market, customer segment, geography, or occasion? Where will you compete?
3. **How to Win** — What is your source of competitive advantage in those chosen spaces?
4. **Core Capabilities** — What organizational skills and capabilities must exist to execute "how to win"?
5. **Management Systems** — What metrics, processes, and structures support those capabilities?

These five choices must be **mutually reinforcing**. A "how to win" that requires design excellence must be backed by capabilities (great design team, research budget) and systems (design review processes, design-to-launch metrics).

**Real example:** P&G applied this framework to reposition Olay as a premium drugstore skincare brand — not competing with luxury (Four Seasons strategy) nor with commodity (cost leadership). The "where to play" was the accessible premium segment; "how to win" was superior efficacy at a price below department stores. Every product formulation, packaging, and shelf placement decision flowed from those choices. The result: over $2B in annual revenue.

## Blue Ocean Strategy

When your market is a red ocean (everyone competing on the same dimensions), Blue Ocean Strategy offers a path to uncontested market space where competition is irrelevant.

Blue Ocean combines cost leadership AND differentiation — not through compromise, but by fundamentally reimagining what the product is.

> Examples: Airbnb (eliminated hotel costs, introduced authentic local stays), Cirque du Soleil (eliminated animal acts and star performers, created theatrical spectacle), Nintendo Switch (eliminated performance race, created social/portable play), Wikipedia (eliminated production costs, introduced community knowledge)

### The Blue Ocean Canvas — ERRC Framework

1. Define 5–12 competing factors in your industry (the things everyone competes on)
2. Plot the dominant industry line — what does the average player offer on each factor?
3. Apply ERRC to your own offer:
   - **Eliminate** — what does the industry take for granted that could be removed entirely?
   - **Reduce** — what factors can we offer well below industry standard?
   - **Raise** — what factors should we raise well above industry standard?
   - **Create** — what factors doesn't the industry offer at all that we could introduce?

The output must be a value curve that looks clearly *different* — not just better — from the industry line. If your curve mirrors competitors' curves, you haven't found a blue ocean.

**Recent examples (2023–2025):**
- **Nintendo Switch:** Eliminated performance competition with Sony/Xbox; created portable social gaming. Result: 137M+ lifetime units sold, 27% global market share.
- **Tesla:** Eliminated dealerships and combustion complexity; created software-defined vehicles. The Model Y became the world's bestselling car across all categories in 2025.
- **Canva:** Eliminated professional design expertise as a requirement; raised template quality and accessibility. Reached $40B valuation by capturing the 90% of people Adobe ignored.

## Competitive Moats: How Design Builds Durability

A moat is a sustainable competitive advantage that prevents competitors from eroding your market position. There are five primary sources:

**1. Switching Costs** — The friction of moving to a competitor. Design creates switching costs through:
- Unique interaction patterns that become habitual
- Deep workflow integrations that would break if you left
- Data and history locked in your product
- Team collaboration features where switching affects the whole organization, not just one user

**2. Network Effects** — The product becomes more valuable as more people use it. Design surfaces this by:
- Showing network activity and size visibly (user counts, activity feeds, recommendations)
- Creating features that only work when teammates are also using the product
- Building trust mechanisms (ratings, reviews, verification) that depend on scale

**3. Intangible Assets** — Brand, distinctive design language, reputation. Apple's design creates pricing power beyond hardware specs. Stripe's documentation quality creates a reputation moat in developer tooling.

**4. Cost Advantage** — Operating at structurally lower cost. Design contributes by reducing support needs through better UX, enabling self-service, and creating efficient onboarding that requires no human touch.

**5. Efficient Scale** — Advantages from size that competitors can't profitably replicate. Design systems and component libraries are a direct expression of efficient scale inside design organizations.

**Design principle:** Don't just design the feature that wins today. Design the moat that prevents competition tomorrow.

## The Innovator's Dilemma: Spotting Disruption

Established companies fail not by doing things wrong, but by doing the right things for the wrong future. They listen to existing customers (who want improvements to existing products), optimize for existing profit margins (which new products disrupt), and ignore markets that seem too small or too low-margin to matter.

Disruption follows a pattern:
1. A new product appears that is **worse** in the dimensions incumbents value, but **better** in dimensions they ignore (price, simplicity, accessibility, convenience)
2. It captures non-consumers or low-end customers that incumbents aren't defending
3. Its performance improves over time, eventually making it competitive in the mainstream
4. By then, the incumbent has lost the structural position to fight back

**Modern examples:**
- Canva disrupted Adobe by building for non-designers (simpler, worse in professional dimensions, better in accessibility). Canva is now $40B.
- Notion disrupted Microsoft Office by starting with personal productivity (worse for enterprise, better for individuals). It's now threatening Microsoft's collaboration dominance.
- Digital photography disrupted Kodak; smartphones disrupted digital cameras — each appeared inferior initially.

**How designers can spot disruption:**
- Watch for "worse is better" products gaining unexpected traction
- Follow non-consumers: who isn't using your category, and what are they doing instead?
- Look for products gaining strong retention despite missing "obvious" features
- Pay attention to segments your company has written off as too small or too price-sensitive

**How established companies should respond:**
Create a separate team with different incentives, different success metrics, and a different physical location — specifically tasked with developing the disruptive technology. The core business will reject it otherwise.

## Strategy as a Design Brief

A well-articulated strategy is one of the most useful design inputs you can have. When you know:
- Where we play (the customer segment, market, and occasion)
- How we win (the source of advantage)
- Which moat we're building (switching costs, network effects, brand)

...you can make design decisions with clarity. You know which features to build, which to ignore, and how to frame every trade-off.

When strategy is unclear in your organization, don't wait — use design methods to make it explicit. Interview stakeholders, map competing strategic bets, sketch different strategic positions, and prototype the trade-offs. Business design's value is making the invisible visible.
