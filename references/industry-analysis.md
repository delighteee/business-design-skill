# Industry Analysis

## When to Use
- Evaluating whether to enter a new market
- Identifying where design can have the biggest strategic impact
- Understanding the forces shaping your industry's profitability
- Framing a design project within the broader business context

---

## The Core Framework: Porter's Five Forces

Industry analysis evaluates five competitive forces that determine how attractive (i.e. profitable) an industry is. The key insight: your product doesn't compete only with direct rivals — it competes with the entire ecosystem.

Always analyze from the perspective of a company **already in the industry** (or wanting to enter it). Rate each force: **Low / Medium / High**. Mostly low = highly attractive. Mostly high = structurally tough.

### The Five Forces

**1. Threat of New Entrants — How hard is it to enter this industry?**
Barriers to entry include: high capital requirements, regulation, brand loyalty, proprietary technology, network effects, economies of scale, and switching costs. Low threat = incumbents are well-protected.

**2. Bargaining Power of Buyers — How easily can customers drive prices down?**
High power = many alternatives, low switching costs, high price sensitivity. Low power = customers are locked in or dependent.

**3. Threat of Substitutes — How else can customers satisfy the same need?**
Think beyond direct alternatives. A car substitutes not just another car brand, but trains, bikes, remote work, and delivery services. High threat = the customer's job can easily be done another way.

**4. Bargaining Power of Suppliers — How easily can suppliers raise prices or reduce quality?**
High power = few suppliers, unique inputs, high switching costs on your side.

**5. Rivalry Among Existing Competitors — How intense is competition?**
High rivalry = many similar-sized players, slow growth, low differentiation, high exit barriers.

## Real-World Case Studies

**Netflix & the Streaming Wars**
Netflix's Five Forces analysis reveals why the streaming industry is structurally difficult: content suppliers hold high bargaining power (studios know Netflix needs hits), substitutes are plentiful (YouTube, gaming, social media, podcasts), and rivalry has intensified dramatically as Disney+, Amazon Prime, and Apple TV+ entered with enormous resources. Netflix's strategic response — investing $18B in original content annually — is a direct consequence of this force map. Own the content, reduce supplier dependency.

**Uber in Ride-Hailing**
Suppliers (drivers) have increasing bargaining power as unionization efforts grow. Buyers (riders) have high power due to low switching costs — Lyft, Bolt, and local taxis are one tap away. Uber's defensible moat is its network density: 94% of rides arrive within 15 minutes vs. 27% for traditional taxis. This makes it structurally superior even in a tough competitive environment.

**Warby Parker in Eyewear**
Industry analysis showed the eyewear market was dominated by a single supplier (one company controlled most brands), keeping retail prices at ~$300 for glasses that cost $10–20 to produce. Warby Parker's design strategy attacked this directly: design in-house (cuts supplier), sell direct-to-consumer (cuts distributor), limit SKUs (cuts complexity). The result: $100 glasses and a $1.75B valuation. The design decisions *flowed from* the industry analysis.

**Apple's Silicon Strategy**
Apple's supplier power analysis showed that Qualcomm held significant leverage over Apple's chip supply — pricing power, delivery timelines, and performance roadmaps were all in Qualcomm's control. Apple's response was to design its own chips (M-series, A-series), eliminating a critical supplier dependency. This wasn't just an engineering decision — it was a Five Forces decision that unlocked better margins and performance differentiation.

## Complementary Framework: PESTEL

Porter's Five Forces analyzes industry-level competitive dynamics. **PESTEL** captures macro-environmental forces that shape those dynamics:

- **Political** — trade policy, government stability, taxation
- **Economic** — interest rates, inflation, consumer spending
- **Social** — cultural shifts, demographics, behavioral change
- **Technological** — new platforms, AI, infrastructure changes
- **Environmental** — sustainability regulations, climate pressures
- **Legal** — data privacy laws, labor regulation, IP frameworks

**Why use both:** Five Forces tells you *who* you compete with and *how intense* that competition is. PESTEL tells you *what macro forces* are shifting those dynamics.

Example: The rise of remote work (Social/Technological in PESTEL) created the conditions for Zoom and Slack to become existential threats to Microsoft's Office suite (Five Forces — new entrant threat surging).

## Industry Lifecycle: How Stage Shapes Design Priorities

The stage of an industry's lifecycle is one of the most powerful — and most ignored — inputs to product design decisions.

| Stage | Characteristics | Design Priority |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Introduction** | Low revenue, negative margins, high R&D | Education, reliability, core value proof |
| **Growth** | Rapid expansion, many new entrants | Onboarding, scalability, platform depth |
| **Shakeout** | Consolidation, slower growth | Differentiation, cost efficiency |
| **Maturity** | Stable revenue, high competition | Retention, incremental innovation, operational UX |
| **Decline** | Falling revenue, exits | Cost reduction, niche focus, migration |

**Smartphones:** The market is in maturity — global shipments declined 4% YoY for four consecutive years. Design priority has shifted from breakthrough features to incremental refinement (foldables represent ~3% of market) and AI-powered differentiation. Every design decision now fights for a shrinking pool of upgraders.

**SaaS (2024–2025):** After a decade of growth-stage investment, SaaS has entered consolidation. Customer acquisition costs rose 55% over three years; 75% of software companies reported declining retention. Design must now prioritize retention, workflow integration (reducing switching costs), and expansion revenue — not just acquisition.

## Jobs-to-Be-Done: Finding Your Real Substitutes

The most important substitutes often don't look like your product at all. Jobs-to-be-done theory reframes competition around the customer's underlying goal, not the product category.

Instead of asking "who else makes what we make?" ask: "What else could a customer use to accomplish the same job?"

**Uber:** The job is "get reliably from point A to B." Substitutes include: taxis, Lyft, bikes, scooters, public transit, walking, owning a car, and working remotely. This JTBD view led Uber to expand into UberEats (different job, same "get me X" structure).

**Slack:** The job is "coordinate my team asynchronously." Substitutes include: email, meetings, shared documents, status calls, project management tools, and even in-person offices. Slack's true competitors are not just Discord and Teams — they're every behavior your team uses to stay aligned.

**Snickers:** The job is "quiet my hunger between meals." Substitutes include: coffee, energy drinks, nuts, fruit, and a full meal. The "You're not you when you're hungry" campaign repositioned Snickers against this entire job, not just the chocolate bar category.

**How to apply it:**
1. Define the job independent of your product ("get me reliably from A to B," not "ride-hailing")
2. List every way a customer could accomplish that job today
3. Ask why they switch between alternatives — what gaps does each leave?
4. Design your product to fulfill the full job, not just beat category competitors

## Turning Industry Analysis into Design Decisions

Once you've mapped the forces, ask:
- Which force is most constraining for our business? That's often where design can unlock the most value.
- Where are incumbents structurally vulnerable? That's a design opportunity.
- What would change the force balance? A new distribution model? A technology shift? A direct-to-consumer play?

The most powerful design decisions aren't about pixels — they're about repositioning where in the value chain the product sits.
