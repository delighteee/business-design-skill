# Pre-Project Business Questions

## When to Use
- At the start of any design project, before diving into research or wireframes
- When joining a project midway and needing to understand the business context quickly
- When a project feels directionless — these questions re-anchor it
- When preparing for stakeholder alignment meetings

---

## Why This Matters

Designers and business stakeholders often operate from completely different mental models of the same project. Starting with these questions surfaces the business context that shapes every design decision, identifies which assumptions are untested, and builds your credibility as someone who thinks beyond the screen.

The goal is not perfect answers — it's identifying which assumptions are untested and which questions the team hasn't aligned on. Those gaps are your biggest risks.

---

## The 13 Questions

### 1. What is the goal of this project — raising revenue or decreasing costs?

Every project should ultimately pull one of these two levers. Map how this project connects to the P&L using a profit tree. Common mistake: teams start work without establishing which lever they're pulling, making success impossible to measure.

### 2. What other projects have already been attempted to achieve this goal?

Why did they succeed or fail? What did the organization learn? This prevents repeating expensive mistakes and helps you inherit useful knowledge rather than starting from scratch. It also reveals whether this problem has already been "solved" — just not implemented correctly.

### 3. What do we want to achieve — and what do we want to learn?

Define success before starting. Set a hypothesis. Identify the main metric. Frame even failed projects as learning opportunities: "We'll know this worked if X metric improves. We'll know it didn't if Y happens." This transforms every project into a test, not just an execution.

### 4. What are the opportunity costs of this project?

What is the most valuable thing your team is NOT doing because it's doing this? If a higher-opportunity alternative exists with the same investment, that's a case for killing or restructuring this project. Teams rarely ask this question explicitly — which is why mediocre projects survive.

### 5. With which other products will customers compare what we're creating?

This reveals your actual competitive landscape — not just industry peers, but every alternative a customer might consider. A fintech payment feature competes with spreadsheets, WhatsApp payment links, and cash, not just competing fintech apps. Use JTBD thinking: what are all the ways a customer could accomplish this job?

### 6. Who else could we soon compete with?

Think beyond current competitors. Large platforms (major tech companies, banks, telecom operators) regularly enter adjacent markets. Could they enter yours? How would you compete if they did? Building design differentiation now — before a well-resourced entrant arrives — is far cheaper than reacting later.

### 7. What is our business model?

How does the company make money? What needs to happen to create the product? Who uses it — and who pays for it? These are often different people. A product designed without understanding the business model often creates friction at exactly the point where money changes hands — undermining the model it's supposed to support.

### 8. How many customers will be using this product at full scale?

A product used by 100 people must be designed very differently from one used by 10 million. Scale determines appropriate design decisions: high-touch concierge model vs. self-service; manual onboarding vs. automated; human support vs. AI. Use numerical prototyping to estimate full scale before you design. (See numerical-prototyping.md)

### 9. What are we uniquely positioned to do?

Every organization has natural advantages: existing customer trust, proprietary data, technology infrastructure, brand recognition, distribution channels, regulatory licenses, geographic presence. What makes the organization structurally better at solving this problem than anyone else? Design should amplify these strengths, not ignore them.

### 10. What is the hypothesis behind this project?

Why does this project exist? What does the team believe to be true? Is that belief validated or assumed? Sometimes the fundamental hypothesis behind a project is so shaky that it should be tested before design work begins. A competitive analysis revealing that a reference competitor is actually in financial difficulty invalidates the entire "copy their model" hypothesis.

### 11. What are the fundamental trade-offs of this project?

Strategy is a series of trade-off decisions. What are the key tensions in this project?
- Fast to market vs. complete feature set?
- Free to access vs. premium quality?
- Simple and opinionated vs. flexible and configurable?
- Broad audience vs. deep niche focus?
- Open platform vs. closed ecosystem?

Name the trade-offs explicitly and lean into them. Ambiguity about trade-offs causes teams to design for everyone — and succeed at nothing.

### 12. What is the business language of this project?

What terms do colleagues outside your team use when talking about this work? (CAC, churn, NPS, ARR, gross margin, unit economics, take rate, activation rate...) Write them down. Look up the ones you don't know. Incorporate them into your project vocabulary. This is how you communicate across functions without losing something in translation.

### 13. Can we kill this project? Can we downsize it?

The most important question, asked last — after you have enough context to evaluate it honestly. Is this project worth doing at all? Could we achieve the same business goal with half the investment? Could we run a smaller experiment first to validate the core hypothesis before committing fully?

If your verdict is "kill it," always come with an alternative. Don't just stop — redirect. "We shouldn't build this feature, but here's what we should build instead" is far more credible and actionable than a critique without a proposal.

## Red Flags to Watch For

**The team can't agree on questions 1, 3, or 7** — fundamental misalignment on goals, success criteria, and business model. This will cause thrash throughout the entire project.

**Questions 10 and 11 have never been discussed** — the project is running on unvalidated assumptions and implicit trade-offs. Decisions will be made inconsistently.

**Question 4 has never been asked** — the team is executing without checking whether this is the highest-leverage use of their time.

**No one has asked question 13** — "build mode" without questioning whether building is the right move.

---

## How to Use These Questions

You don't need all 13 answered before starting. But you do need to know which ones are unanswered — those are your biggest risks.

Bring these questions into your kick-off workshops. Frame them not as gatekeeping ("we can't start until we have answers") but as accelerators ("an hour spent on these saves weeks of misaligned work later").

If you're a designer on a project where no one has asked these questions, raise them yourself. This is what it means to operate as a business designer — not waiting for a product manager or strategy team to define the context, but actively creating clarity for the team.
