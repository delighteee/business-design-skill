# Business Design Skill

Business design is the practice of applying design methodologies and mindset to business challenges —
ensuring that what we create is not just desirable and feasible, but also **viable**. It is the bridge
between the designer's instinct for human needs and the executive's mandate for business results.

**Business design uses:**
1. Design methodologies (design thinking, iteration, abductive reasoning, prototyping)
2. Design mindset (customer-centricity, thinking in extremes, embracing ambiguity, visual thinking)
3. Business tools (Porter's Five Forces, Ecosystem Map, financial projections, etc.)
4. Applied to business challenges (growth, strategy, business models, cost, organization, product)

It is **not** management consulting (deductive, profit-first), not product management (product-scoped),
not service design (lacks viability lens), and not business development (sales-focused, no design methodology).

---

## Six Mindsets of a Business Designer

1. **Start with customers and stakeholders** — begin every challenge by talking to people, not spreadsheets
2. **Think in extremes** — extreme prototypes and scenarios surface ideas conventional thinking misses
3. **Prototype to learn** — financial projections and business model sketches are learning vehicles, not just proof
4. **Combine qualitative + quantitative** — pair interview insights with macro data
5. **Embrace small data sets** — ten interviews are enough to generate the first round of hypotheses
6. **Think visually** — turn business data into maps, charts, and canvases that reveal patterns

## The Business Design Process

Non-linear — adapt to the challenge:

| Stage | What Happens |
|-------|-------------|
| **Empathize & Explore** | Customer + stakeholder interviews; desk research on industry & competitors |
| **Define** | Synthesize learnings; reframe the real challenge |
| **Ideate** | HMW questions; brainstorm; cluster into opportunity areas |
| **Prototype** | Business model sketches, financial projections, process maps, pricing strategies |
| **Test** | Set hypotheses; run experiments; track the right metrics |

## Pre-Project Business Questions

Ask these before any design project begins. They surface the business context that shapes every decision and identify which assumptions are untested.

1. **What is the goal — raising revenue or decreasing costs?** Every project ultimately pulls one of these two levers. Map how it connects to the P&L.
2. **What has already been attempted?** Why did it succeed or fail? Don't repeat the same mistakes.
3. **What do we want to achieve and learn?** Define success. Set a hypothesis and a main metric. Even failed projects are valuable if framed as learning.
4. **What are the opportunity costs?** What is the most valuable thing we're NOT doing because we're doing this?
5. **With which products will customers compare ours?** Reveals the actual competitive landscape.
6. **Who else could we soon compete with?** Large platforms (tech, banks, telco) enter adjacent markets constantly.
7. **What is our business model?** How do we make money? Who uses the product — and who pays for it?
8. **How many customers will use this at full scale?** 100 users vs. 10 million users demand fundamentally different design decisions.
9. **What are we uniquely positioned to do?** Identify the organization's natural advantages — data, trust, technology, distribution — and amplify them.
10. **What is the hypothesis behind this project?** Is it validated or assumed? Sometimes the hypothesis should be tested before any design begins.
11. **What are the fundamental trade-offs?** Fast vs. deliberate? Free vs. premium? Broad vs. specialized? Name them explicitly and lean in.
12. **What is the business language of this project?** Learn the terms colleagues outside your team use (CAC, churn, NPS, ARR...) and incorporate them.
13. **Can we kill or downsize this project?** The most important question. If yes, always come with an alternative.

## Industry Analysis — Porter's Five Forces

Use when evaluating a new market or identifying where design has the most strategic leverage. Always analyze from the perspective of a company already in (or entering) the industry.

**Threat of New Entrants** — How hard is it to enter? Barriers include: capital requirements, regulation, brand loyalty, proprietary technology, network effects, economies of scale. Low threat = incumbents are protected.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers** — Can customers easily drive prices down? High power = many alternatives, low switching costs.

**Threat of Substitutes** — How else can customers meet the same need? Think beyond direct alternatives (e.g., remote work substitutes commuting, not just a competing transit app).

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers** — Can suppliers raise prices or reduce quality easily? High power = few suppliers, unique inputs.

**Rivalry Among Existing Competitors** — How intense is competition? High rivalry = many similar-sized players, slow growth, low differentiation.

**Scoring:** Rate each force Low / Medium / High. Mostly low = highly attractive industry. Mostly high = structurally tough to be profitable in.

**Design connection:** The most constraining force is often where design can unlock the most value. Warby Parker attacked supplier power by designing in-house and selling direct — design decisions that flowed directly from industry analysis.

## Competitor Analysis

Use when starting a new product or feature, or when differentiating your product in a crowded market.

**Step 1 — Identify competitors:**
- **Direct** — offer the same product or service
- **Indirect** — offer a different product that solves the same underlying problem
- **Future** — large platforms that could enter your market; design defensibility against them now

Aim for 4–10 competitors, a mix of direct and indirect.

**Step 2 — Gather data across four buckets:**

*Arena:* Industry/sub-industry, market size, growth trajectory, dominant clusters and players.

*Business:* Founding year, revenue model, estimated revenue, funding, revenue trend, market share, profitability, user base size.

*Product:* Core competitive advantage, product portfolio, feature set, pricing model and price points.

*Customer:* Primary target group, the user problem they solve, customer reviews (App Store, Google Play, Trustpilot, G2, Reddit) — what people love and what they complain about.

**Useful sources:** Competitor website, Meta Ad Library, Wikipedia, Crunchbase, Google News, annual reports (public companies), Statista, app store reviews.

**Step 3 — Synthesize:** Skim and color-code related data points. Plot competitors on 2×2 matrices (e.g., price vs. quality). Look for whitespace — what everyone offers that customers don't actually value, and what no one offers that customers actually want. Customer complaints are design opportunities.

## Business Strategy

Use when defining a product's positioning, making trade-off decisions, or explaining why design choices were made.

**Strategy is NOT** a goal, a set of best practices, or simply a plan. **Strategy IS** a series of deliberate trade-off decisions; a quest for sustainable competitive advantage; choosing what to do AND what not to do; about being genuinely *different*, not incrementally better.

> "The essence of strategy is choosing what not to do." — Michael Porter

### Porter's Three Competitive Strategies

**Cost Leadership** — Beat competitors by offering the lowest prices through efficiency and scale. Design implication: simplicity, standardization, frictionless self-service. (IKEA, Walmart, McDonald's)

**Differentiation** — Win by being uniquely valuable; charge a premium because customers can't get this elsewhere. Design implication: exceptional experience, distinctive brand, features competitors can't easily copy. (Apple, BMW, Four Seasons)

**Focus** — Serve a specific customer segment better than anyone else, either through cost focus or differentiation focus within that niche. (Porsche, LinkedIn, specialist B2B tools)

**The strategic sin:** Getting stuck in the middle — trying to be both low-cost and highly differentiated without committing to either.

### Blue Ocean Strategy

Escape overcrowded markets by creating new market space where competition is irrelevant. Combines cost leadership AND differentiation by fundamentally reimagining the product. (Airbnb, Skype, Wikipedia)

**Blue Ocean Canvas — ERRC Framework:**
1. Define 5–12 competing factors in your industry
2. Plot the dominant industry line (what the average player offers)
3. Apply ERRC: What can we **Eliminate**? **Reduce** below industry standard? **Raise** above it? **Create** that the industry doesn't offer at all?

The result must be a clearly different value curve — not just a better version of the existing one.

## Business Models — The Ecosystem Map

Use when designing a new product or service, or when evaluating whether a design decision aligns with how the business captures value.

Business model innovation has greater impact on business success than product innovation alone. A product's UX is not just how someone uses it — it's also how they pay, upgrade, share, and stop using it.

**The Ecosystem Map** makes abstract "how we make money" questions concrete and visual.

**Four building blocks:**
- **Actors** — everyone involved: your company, customers, partners, suppliers, distributors, third parties
- **Flow of Information** — what data moves between actors, and how?
- **Flow of Goods** — how does the product travel from creator to customer?
- **Flow of Money** — who pays whom, how much, when, and in what form?

**Three parts of every business model:**

| Part | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Value Creation** | How the company produces what it offers (left side of map) |
| **Value Delivery** | How the product reaches customers (lower section) |
| **Value Capture** | How the business gets paid (upper right — customers and revenue flows) |

**Common business model patterns designers should know:**
- **Subscription** — recurring revenue; design toward retention and habit formation
- **Marketplace/Platform** — connects two user groups; design must serve multiple user types simultaneously
- **Freemium** — free tier must be genuinely valuable; upgrade must be clearly worthwhile
- **Advertising** — users are the product; creates tension between user wellbeing and business model
- **Razor & Blades** — cheap primary product; profit on consumables/add-ons
- **Direct Sales** — design drives conversion at each funnel stage

## Numerical Prototyping (Discovery Driven Planning)

Use when evaluating the financial viability of an idea before building, or when making a business case for a design investment.

Just as designers create visual prototypes to test desirability, we create **financial prototypes** to test viability. The goal is not to predict the future — it's to surface the assumptions the business is betting on, so the riskiest ones can be tested first.

**The DDP Process:**

1. **Start with the end goal** — state a specific, measurable target (e.g., "$100k yearly profit")
2. **Work backwards** — use simple math to calculate what must be true at every level
   > $100k profit → $500k revenue at 20% margin → $41,666/month → $1,388/day → 92 customers/day at $15 avg
3. **Document every assumption** — every number you calculated is an assumption. List them all with a confidence rating (High / Medium / Low)
4. **Rank by risk** — low-confidence + high-impact assumptions are the ones that could kill the business if wrong
5. **Design a testing plan** — create the smallest, cheapest test for each high-risk assumption

**For design projects inside existing products — the Profit Tree:**
Every significant design change either increases revenue or decreases costs. Work out the math explicitly:
> "Improving onboarding completion from 40% → 55% with 10,000 monthly signups = 1,500 more activated users/month. At $30 LTV = $45,000/month additional revenue."

**Avoid:** Using DDP to *prove* your idea works. Use it to *find out* whether it can, and under what conditions.

## Design Metrics

Use when defining success for a product or feature, or when building credibility with data-skeptical stakeholders.

**Design metrics** measure value created *for users*. **Business metrics** measure value *captured by the business*. A great design metric predicts business metric improvement.

**How to find your design metric (two steps):**
1. What does success look like for our users? (The core value proposition)
2. What is the ONE action a user must take to actually extract that value?

**The metric quality ladder** (Google Calendar example):

| Level | Metric | Why It's Better |
|-------|--------|----------------|
| OK | New events created per week | Goes up just from adding users — not meaningful |
| Good | New events created per week per user | Per-user, but averages hide inactivity |
| Great | % of users who create at least one event per week | Shows what share of users take the key action |
| Excellent | % of users who create 3+ events per week | Threshold tied to real value; comparable week-over-week |

Pattern: move from totals → per-user → binary (did they or didn't they) → threshold (did they enough to get real value).

**Four criteria for a good metric:**
1. **Understandable** — you can explain it in one sentence
2. **Comparable** — can be compared across time, segments, or competitors
3. **Actionable** — when it moves, you know what design change to make (avoid vanity metrics)
4. **Normalized** — expressed as a ratio or rate, not a raw total

**Design Metrics Canvas:**
- **Main metric** — the one number that defines design success
- **Supporting metrics** — secondary signals that explain *why* the main metric moved
- **Counter metrics** — what could go wrong if you over-optimize the main metric
- **Business metric link** — explicit connection to the metric your stakeholders care about

Set the metric *before* you design. Establish a baseline before you ship. Disagree with bad metrics out loud and propose better ones.

## Business Metrics

Use when presenting design work to executives, making a business case, or connecting design impact to financial outcomes.

Executives primarily care about: increasing revenue, decreasing costs, acquiring new customers, extracting more value from existing customers, and shareholder value. Lead with design impact, then immediately translate into whichever of these applies.

**Revenue metrics:**

*ROI* — (Value gained − Cost of investment) / Cost of investment. Make this calculation explicit in every design proposal.

*MRR / ARR* — Monthly/Annual Recurring Revenue. For subscription businesses; shows stable revenue trajectory.

*ARPU* — Average Revenue Per User. Improving upsell flows and pricing UX lifts ARPU.

*LTV* — Lifetime Value = ARPU × average customer lifespan. Improving retention directly increases LTV.

**Cost metrics:**

*CAC* — Customer Acquisition Cost = (Marketing + sales spend) / New customers acquired. A good business maintains LTV:CAC ≥ 3:1.

*Fixed vs. Variable Costs* — Fixed costs don't change with volume (rent, salaries). Variable costs scale with output (transaction fees, shipping). Digital products with near-zero marginal cost are structurally powerful.

**Customer base metrics:**

*DAU / WAU / MAU* — Active users per day/week/month. Design that improves engagement and habit formation lifts these.

*DAU/MAU Ratio (Stickiness)* — What % of monthly users return daily? Social apps aim for 50%+; most SaaS aims for 15–25%.

*Churn Rate* — Customers lost / Customers at start of period. Target for SaaS: ~0.5–1% monthly (5–7% annually). Churn is often a design problem.

*NPS* — % Promoters (9–10) minus % Detractors (0–6). World-class NPS: 70+.

**Design decision → business impact table:**

| Design Action | Business Outcome |
|--------------|-----------------|
| Better onboarding → more users activate | ↑ Conversion, ↓ effective CAC |
| Smoother upgrade flow → more users go paid | ↑ MRR, ↑ ARPU |
| Self-service UX → fewer support tickets | ↓ Variable costs |
| Better habit design → higher retention | ↓ Churn, ↑ LTV |
| Features users actually use → higher engagement | ↑ Stickiness, ↑ NPS |
| Frictionless checkout → fewer abandoned purchases | ↑ Revenue |

## Economics Principles for Product Decisions

**Economies of Scale** — As you produce more, cost per unit falls. Fixed costs spread across more units. Minimum Efficient Scale is the volume below which you're structurally disadvantaged against larger competitors.

*Zero Marginal Cost* — When each additional user costs almost nothing to serve (software, digital content), growth compounds extraordinarily. This is the structural advantage of digital products.

*Network Effects* — When more users make the product more valuable for everyone, you get a flywheel that compounds economies of scale and builds a structural moat competitors can't replicate with features alone.

**Diseconomies of Scale** — Past an optimal size, coordination and management costs start to rise again. Processes that work at 10 people break at 100.

**Economies of Scope** — It's cheaper to produce related products together than separately because of shared infrastructure, brand, or distribution. This is often the logic behind product portfolio expansions and acquisitions.

**Opportunity Cost** — The value of the next best alternative given up when making a choice. Every design decision has an opportunity cost — the things you didn't build. Ask: "If we weren't working on this, what would we be doing?" If the alternative is clearly more valuable, reconsider.

**Time Value of Money** — Money today is worth more than the same amount in the future. Value created sooner is worth more than the same value created later. This is why speed of iteration matters financially, not just competitively.

**Supply and Demand** — Highly differentiated products face less elastic demand (customers pay more because there's no close substitute). Commodity products face very elastic demand (small price increases send customers to competitors). Design can shift demand curves — a better UX or stronger brand reduces price sensitivity.

**Principal-Agent Problem** — When an agent's incentives don't align with a principal's interests, you get misaligned behavior. Map these relationships in any multi-stakeholder product. Ask: whose incentives are misaligned, and does the design reinforce or mitigate that?
